Why Crochet?

Crochet is the process of turning yarn, thread, and other materials into textiles using a single hook.

The most obvious difference between crochet and knitting is the use of a single hook for crochet as opposed to two (or sometimes more) needles for knitting. While that alone may convince you that crochet is the simpler and better choice of handcraft for you, below is a list of crochet pros and cons to help you decide if crochet is right for you.

Pros:

Easier to pick up and put down
Crochet uses a single hook, which means once your stitch is made, it is secure as long as you don't pull on your working yarn. In knitting, every stitch is kept on the needles, and if your work falls off the needles, you have to carefully pick each one back up. You'll likely drop a stitch, and have to undo some of your work or even start over. With crochet, if your hook falls out you simply reinsert your hook into the last working loop and keep going!
Quicker to work up
Crochet works up much faster than knitting. Speed varies by person, and can also depend on your tools (ergonomic hooks can also increase speed), but crochet is widely accepted to be faster. All evidence is anecdotal but crocheting a blanket in a couple of days is commonplace, while knitting a blanket in the same amount of time is unheard of.
Easier to fix mistakes
Mistakes are bound to happen in anything you do, but thankfully fixing crochet mistakes is super easy! With one working stitch, all you have to do is pull on your working end to "frog" your work (the sound frogging makes sounds like a ribbit!) back to before your mistake, and you're good to go again. With so many working loops in knitting, frogging is unlikely to work very well. You'll have to "tink", or knit backwards until you've undone your mistake. Again, more time consuming!
More versatile
You can do almost anything with crochet! Amigurumi is the practice of creating stuffed toys- it's ridiculously easy to make your own stuffed animals with just a crochet hook and a single stitch (single crochet). Hand knitted toys are far more difficult, and there are less knit patterns for stuffed toys because knitting is just not the best way to make them. Crochet also allows you to easily create baskets, bags, and more, while the fabric that knitting creates is simply thinner and less sturdy than crocheted fabric, and usually works up flat, so you'll have to sew pieces together if you want a bag. Crochet, on the other hand, works both flat and in the round with ease.
Cons:
Uses more yarn
It is widely accepted that crochet uses about 30% more yarn than knitting. This can be difficult for those with budget constraints, or who are bad at buying enough yarn at once for a project (me, in both cases). However, knitting requires more tools, as each pair of needles is stuck to one project at a time, while a single hook can be used across several projects. Anyone who does yarn crafts or knows someone who does also knows that pretty quickly having to stop yourself from buying new yarn is the bigger issue than needing more of it. If you want to use less yarn, crochet might not be for you, but it's a great way to work through a big stash of yarn.
Not as good for colorwork
While crochet can make a much wider variety of things, it does lose to knitting in the area of colorwork. There are may different techniques for changing colors in crochet, but none of them give as clean of a look as knitted color changes do.
Not everyone's aesthetic
While crochet's thicker fabric makes it more versatile, some people prefer the look of knitted fabric, or find crocheted fabric to be ugly.
More to learn
Crochet has so many stitches you can learn that many people own a "stitch bible", or a huge list of different crochet stitches. Knitting, on the other hand, only has a handful of possible stitches in comparison. While some may like the possbilities of so many sittches to learn, others may be overwhelmed by the sheer number. Fortunately, you only need to know 1-3 stitches to crochet a wide variety of things!

It all comes down to a matter of preference, but crochet is versatile, easier to work with, and quick to learn!
